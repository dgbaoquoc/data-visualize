Why Donald Trump is people’s all-time favorite memes?

Donald Trump has transformed as a result of his Internet presence. In 2016, he was the internet's candidate—listed he's in the Urban Dictionary's definition of meme god—and his campaign used Facebook advertising to defeat Hillary Clinton. Since then, he's gotten even more concerned with small complaints and conspiracy theories that play well on Twitter, which is utilized by just 22% of the population in the United States.

Trump has also had a noticeable impact on the internet. During President Trump's first term, Americans saw his administration savor the chance to repeal net neutrality, the foundational concept of free and open internet. “Mr. President”, on the other hand, has had a far greater influence on the internet than the strangest memes or the longest Twitter feuds. His administration has altered how Americans connect with one another online, amplifying the tone of division and distrust, molding conventions and standards, and instilling the expectation that each day online will be stranger than the last.

“Meme-god” enters the game.

Vic Berger, a Vine artist, created creepy six-second films during the 2016 presidential primary season, exposing Republican hopefuls as ludicrous. He'd focus on the moments when they appeared to be the most genuine versions of themselves: Jeb Bush was a tormented husk of a failed politician who knew words but not human face emotions. Trump kept repeating statements and making strange physical motions, such as pointing with his thumbs at individuals. There were a lot of air horns on the scene. The New Yorker dubbed Berger a "political satirist for the internet election" after millions of people viewed each tape and expressed their delight in the comments.

Trump has indirectly promoted such conduct and has occasionally gladly engaged in the worst of meme culture. Memes helped him win in 2016, and now other politicians are expected to follow suit: Memes are just another type of political advertisement that can be bought. Michael Bloomberg's team spent a lot of money on advertising on prominent meme accounts when he ran for the Democratic nomination this year, claiming the need to "meet people where they are and fight with President Trump's tremendous digital operation."
